Add to list UX case study

Completed while at Flipp

Overview

This project aimed to improve the clipping experience and strengthen its connection to the shopping list for better user outcomes.

57% of MAU clip items, but only 38% navigate to the shopping list, revealing a disconnect. Users struggled with clipping awareness, unclear feedback, and finding clipped items in a busy list.

The greatest opportunity was converting engaged visits into EV+Clip sessions by encouraging clipping during shopping sessions and making the relationship between clipped items and lists more intuitive.

What is Flipp?

Flipp is a shopping platform that lets users discover deals, coupons, and flyers from local retailers to save money on products.

Problem

Adding items to a list across the app can be challenging. While it seems straightforward, users often struggle to complete the action successfully.

The add interaction lacked clarity, failed to show where items went after being added, and created inconsistencies that hindered effective shopping list management.

Goal

  • Increase clipping awareness: enhance visibility and discoverability of the clipping functionality across the app.
  • Provide clear feedback: introduce user feedback that clearly indicates where clipped items are saved.
  • Strengthen user understanding: educate users on the connection between clipping and their shopping list through intuitive design and prompts.

Process

The process moved through define, research, ideate, test, refine, and ship. To assess the significant design changes, I conducted two rounds of unmoderated user testing.

This approach provided valuable insights and confidence for further refinement and development.

UX explorations

The UX explored clearer add states, item quantity controls, list-level feedback, and confirmation patterns that help users understand when items have been added successfully.

Outcome

  • High completion rates: all users (100%) successfully added items from search results and in-flyer tasks, with average satisfaction ratings of 6.8–6.9 out of 7.
  • Positive feedback on visual elements: animations, checkmarks, and number badges were widely appreciated for confirming actions, with 17/20 users noticing these indicators in the in-flyer task.
  • Ease of removing items: users consistently identified multiple ways to remove items from their list, showcasing clarity in the design.
  • Expanded actions well understood: users instinctively expanded the bottom sheet to access additional actions like share and easily located options to adjust quantities or remove items.

Reflections

  • Initially, we considered a full revamp of the swipe and flyer viewing experience. However, exploring various options showed that a redesign was not essential. Instead, I leveraged native components and common practices to solve the problem.
  • I was confident skipping usability testing before the experiment due to the use of common design practices and components, which positively impacted development time and user interaction.
